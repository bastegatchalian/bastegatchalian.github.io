Honest Assessment

You're Learning
Too Much.

Cut These. Right Now.

These techniques aren't worth a single drill rep in the next 50 days. They require timing, sensitivity, or positional awareness you haven't built yet.

  • cut  Scissor sweep — setup is slow, better in IBJJF points
  • cut  Mount armbar — too many moving parts under pressure
  • cut  Mount kimura/americana — you don't finish from mount yet
  • cut  Triangle choke — hip angle takes months to ingrain
  • cut  Cross collar choke — grip fighting from guard is unreliable
  • cut  Pendulum/flower sweep — too much coordination, too little return

Keep Only These 5 Sequences

  • keep  Collar tie → snap → go-behind → RNC
  • keep  Sprawl → front headlock → spin to back → RNC
  • keep  Side control → back take when they turn → RNC
  • keep  Hip bump sweep → side control → back take → RNC
  • keep  Closed guard kimura → finish or sweep
The Rule

Every sequence ends the same way. Collar tie ends at RNC. Sprawl ends at RNC. Side control ends at RNC. This is not a coincidence — it's the entire strategy.

01
Rule Set Intelligence

Sub-Only
Is Different.

What No Longer Applies

  • Holding side control to "bank points" — worthless here
  • "Score first" mentality — there is no scoreboard
  • Defending takedowns just to stay standing — no takedown points
  • Stalling from top position — it only costs you energy
  • Protecting a lead — there is no lead to protect

The Shift

In points-based grappling, position = winning. In sub-only, position only matters if it moves you closer to a finish. Everything you do must answer: "Does this help me submit them, or stop them from submitting me?"

What Sub-Only Gives You

  • Freedom to pursue subs without fear of losing position points
  • You can give up top position if it leads to a better submission angle
  • You can disengage from bad scrambles without conceding points
  • Your opponent has no incentive to stall either — they must engage
  • 10 minutes is enough time to be patient and deliberate

The New Guiding Question

At every moment in the match, ask: "Is this position threatening a submission?" If not, improve the position or disengage and reset.

Pacing Strategy

How To Use
10 Minutes

0:002:004:006:008:0010:00
■ ESTABLISH (0–2 min) ■ HUNT (2–5 min) ■ FINISH (5–8 min) ■ SURVIVE / RESET (8–10)

0–2 Min: Establish

  • Get your collar tie — don't let them dictate range
  • Feel them out: do they shoot? Do they hand-fight back?
  • Controlled aggression — not explosions, not stalling
  • First snap and go-behind attempt here
  • Don't gas yourself trying to finish in the first 90 seconds

2–5 Min: Hunt

  • You now know their tendencies — exploit them
  • Commit to positional sequences, not random attacks
  • Get to back or side control — build your submission platform
  • If you have back control: this is when you finish the RNC

5–8 Min: Finish

  • Both athletes are tired — transitions open up more
  • If you have a dominant position: hunt the submission now
  • Their defense slows as fatigue sets in — be patient
  • Don't rush — a tight seatbelt and body triangle can wait them out

8–10 Min: Survive or Reset

  • If you have nothing: stand up, reset to collar tie, work fast
  • If they have nothing: hold your position, make them work
  • If neither has a sub: see the overtime slide
  • Don't panic-scramble — controlled chaos is better than frantic chaos
04
Energy Management

Explode or
Wait?

When to Explode

  • The go-behind attempt — full burst, short duration
  • Sprawling a shot — instant hip drive, not sustained effort
  • When their base is compromised mid-scramble
  • When you feel the RNC close — cinch it hard and fast
  • First 30 seconds of any scramble — commit or disengage
The Burst Rule

Explosions last 3–5 seconds. If you haven't secured the position in 5 seconds, stop exploding and settle into control. Long sustained explosions = you gas out, they wait.

When to Stay Patient

  • When you have seatbelt on the back — sit there, breathe, tighten slowly
  • When you have side control and they're not moving — heavy pressure, no energy wasted
  • When hand-fighting — efficiency over force
  • When they're defending well — stop, reset, try again fresher
  • Minutes 3–5 — let them burn energy defending, you conserve

When to Disengage

  • Scramble has gone past 10 seconds with no clear advantage
  • You feel yourself burning out — stand up and reset
  • They're countering everything in a bottom sequence
  • In sub-only: resetting costs you nothing. No one scores.

06
Opponent Intelligence

The MMA
Wrestler Problem

Their Strengths

  • Explosive double and single leg shots
  • Athletic top control — heavy smothering pressure
  • Aggression and forward pressure from the start
  • Conditioning from MMA training — they're used to 5-min rounds
  • May know basic leg locks and guillotines from MMA camp
Their Weakness

MMA wrestlers are trained to control, not to submit. Their top control is for ground-and-pound, not for BJJ finishes. If you neutralize the takedown, they often don't know what to do from standing. Their submission offense from top is usually limited to basic arm attacks.

How You Beat Them

  • Deny the double leg with your sprawl — this is your first line
  • After the sprawl: don't let them reset — go to back immediately
  • Collar tie and head control cancels most of their wrestling offense
  • If they get top: face them, frame hard, don't flatten out
  • Wrestle up immediately — don't accept extended bottom time

Accepting Bottom Position

  • Sub-only means bottom is not automatic loss — but guard is still dangerous
  • Accept bottom only if you have a submission threat from there
  • If you have closed guard: you have kimura and hip bump — use them
  • If you're in a bad guard position: stand up immediately
  • Never accept bottom with no attack plan — that's just surviving

  • No — if you're giving up back control to chase an armbar from mount

  • Against a bigger wrestler: don't war for the takedown — use their momentum
  • Redirect is better than a power struggle at the same level

03
Standing Game

Collar Tie
Is Everything

The Collar Tie System

  • Get the collar tie before they can — set the agenda
  • Head control = shot control. Their head goes down → you own them
  • Snap down → they react → redirect → go-behind: your entire standing game
  • Single leg: short, tight, stay close to the body. Don't reach.
  • If you get the single: don't fight for the finish — switch to go-behind

Takedown Defense

  • Watch their hips drop — that's the signal, not their hands
  • Sprawl: hips forward and down, chest heavy on their head
  • Front headlock: clamp the head immediately after sprawl
  • Walk your hips around → spin to the back → seatbelt

Should You Wrestle Hard?

Sub-Only Answer

You don't need the takedown to win. You need to get to the back from anywhere. A collar tie that sets up a go-behind is better than a power double that burns 30 seconds of energy and lands you in a scramble.

Guillotine Awareness

  • On collar tie: keep your head up and posture strong
  • On single leg: head inside, not outside — outside = guillotine setup
  • If they catch a guillotine: don't pull your head out with force — walk to the same side as the arm, drive through
  • Tap early if tight. Reset and go again.
09
The Submission Platform

Back Control
= Your Finish

Seatbelt — Lock It First

  • Top arm: over the shoulder, across the chest — this is the choke arm
  • Bottom arm: under the armpit — this is the control arm
  • Clasp hands together. Chest to their back. Never let this go.
  • Get on your side — not flat on your back, never flat on your back

Hooks or Body Triangle

  • Hooks (feet inside their thighs): standard. Get one first, then the second.
  • Body triangle: if they fight the hooks. Cross your legs around their waist.
  • Both are submission platforms — the RNC works from either.
  • Hooks are faster to get. Body triangle is harder to escape.

RNC — Step by Step

  • Top arm sneaks under the chin — slow and patient
  • Don't force the chin — wait for them to speak (chin moves up)
  • Once under: bottom hand grips your own bicep
  • Top hand presses the back of their head
  • Expand your chest, break their posture back — the choke is your whole body
The Patient Finish

In sub-only you have 10 minutes. If you have the seatbelt and they're defending the choke — that is fine. Stay there. They're burning energy defending. Your grip is resting. The finish comes when they stop defending. You can hold this for 3 minutes.

10
Top Game

Side Control
Is A Springboard

Side Control in Sub-Only

Side control is not the finish — it's the setup. Your goal isn't to hold it for points. Your goal is to use it to force the back take or execute the kimura.

  • Heavy pressure: chest on chest, crossface under the jaw
  • Threaten the kimura — grab their wrist, make them react
  • When they turn away: back take immediately — this is automatic
  • When they bridge and roll: kimura or americana
  • When they stay still: build kimura or improve to mount
Don't Just Lay There

In sub-only, stalling from side control wastes time you could be hunting a finish. Stay heavy AND stay active with submission threats. Make them react.

Kimura from Side Control

  • Secure their wrist, hip in tight to isolate the arm
  • Figure-4 the shoulder — sit up and rotate the shoulder back
  • This is a finish in sub-only — not just a sweep setup
  • If they defend by rolling: don't let go — kimura from mount or turtle

Mount in Sub-Only

  • Mount has value: use it to force back exposure
  • High mount → they bench press you → you switch to RNC angle
  • Don't hunt armbar from mount unless it's completely obvious
  • Armbar from mount = you give up mount position if they defend = bad trade in sub-only
  • Mount → back take is the sequence. Stay disciplined.
11
Bottom Game

Bottom Position
= Danger Zone

The Sub-Only Bottom Rule

Important Shift

In points, accepting bottom position costs you 2 or 4 points. In sub-only, it costs you nothing — except your safety. The danger isn't losing points. The danger is getting submitted from bottom. Accept bottom only if you have a plan.

From Closed Guard

  • You have 2 weapons: kimura and hip bump sweep
  • Break their posture constantly — no upright posture = no guard pass
  • When they post their hand: kimura immediately
  • When they sit up: hip bump → side control → back
  • If neither is working: open guard and stand up

Under Side Control

  • Never turn away — back exposure is worse than side control
  • Frame on their hip and their jaw — never on their throat
  • Shrimp: drive hips out, then pull knees in to recover guard
  • Half guard → look to take the back or stand up
  • If you've been under for 30 seconds with no progress: force a stand-up

When to Immediately Stand Up

  • Any time you're in a loose control situation with no sub threat on them
  • If they have no seatbelt and no arm isolated
  • In sub-only: standing up costs nothing — no takedown concession
  • Post hand, get to one knee, cover your neck as you rise
  • Back to collar tie — back to your game
12
Survival

Recognize It
Early. Tap Early.

Armbar

Early sign: They control your wrist and their hip rises under your elbow.

Defense: Stack your weight on them, posture up hard, pull your elbow to your centerline.

If tight: Grab your own wrist (no thumb), turn thumb up toward ceiling, stack and rotate out.

Tap before it straightens. Joint damage isn't worth it.

Kimura / Americana

Early sign: They have a figure-4 on your shoulder. You feel the rotation starting.

Kimura defense: Grip your belt. Turn into them. Roll toward their elbow, not away.

Americana defense: Never let your arm go flat to the mat. Elbow close to your hip always.

The shoulder doesn't give warning. Tap before you feel pain.

RNC + Guillotine

RNC early sign: Their arm is sneaking under your chin. Two-hand grip immediately.

RNC defense: Tuck chin down, grab the choking arm with both hands, spin into them, walk down their body.

Guillotine: Head inside on your collar tie. If caught: same side as the arm, drive through and up. Never pull your head out directly.

Tap to chokes before your vision goes. No hero moments.

The Sub-Only Tap Rule

In a 10-minute match you might get caught early. Tap immediately, reset, and use the remaining 8 minutes to win. Refusing to tap and getting injured ends your competition day. In sub-only, surviving round 1 to fight again is the right call.

13
Leg Lock Safety

Stay Clear
Of The Legs

What NOT To Do

  • Sit into leg entanglements with no leg lock knowledge
  • Panic-spin when they have your leg — spinning creates heel hook angles
  • Fight a heel hook that's already locked — tap immediately
  • Extend your leg when they're hunting it
  • Assume sub-only white belt = no leg attacks

MMA Fighter Warning

MMA fighters train straight ankle locks from ground-and-pound positioning. They may not know advanced leg locks, but a straight ankle is basic enough to catch you. Stay standing as long as possible.

Safety Rules

  • Keep toes pointing away from them when on the ground
  • If they grab your ankle: stand up immediately — step over and away
  • Straight ankle lock escape: point toes up and out, pull your knee to your chest
  • If you feel knee pressure: tap immediately — knees are the most commonly injured joint
  • Your best leg defense: stay on your feet

If They Pull Guard or Sit Down

  • Don't dive in without assessing their leg positioning
  • Stand over them, control their knees, don't sit into them
  • If you can't pass standing: disengage, reset to standing
  • In sub-only, standing over them costs you nothing
14
Late Match Intelligence

Neither Person
Has Submitted

Minutes 7–10 — The Reset Phase

Both athletes are tired. Defenses are slowing. This is not the time to desperation scramble — it's the time to be more deliberate, not more chaotic.

  • Stand up if you're stuck in a bad position
  • Return to collar tie — your most practiced position
  • Look for the snap and go-behind — fresh attempt from standing
  • Their gas tank is also low — they may give you the go-behind more easily
  • One clean back take at minute 8 wins the match
Fatigue Is Your Friend

A tired person defends the RNC slower. A tired person's collar tie grip breaks faster. The same go-behind you've been drilling for 50 days becomes easier to hit when they're exhausted at minute 8. Stay patient. Stay technical.

If It Goes to Overtime

Different events handle overtime differently. Dog Fight CDO may use a coin flip to assign position, or additional rounds. Whatever the format:

  • If given a position: always choose back if offered. Always.
  • If given a choice of top or bottom: choose top — you have side control to back take
  • If given a standing restart: back to collar tie immediately
  • Overtime favors the athlete with the cleaner game plan — that's you

Psychological Edge Late

  • They're thinking "I haven't submitted anyone" — their confidence is shaky
  • You're thinking "I know exactly what I'm going to do" — that's an edge
  • Controlled breathing between exchanges signals confidence
  • Don't show frustration — your face is your poker face

Mental Preparation

Inside
The Match

Adrenaline Dump — What to Expect

  • Heart rate spikes before you even step on the mat — normal
  • Hands shake, vision narrows, time distorts
  • Box breathing: 4 counts in → 4 hold → 4 out → 4 hold
  • Repeat 5–6 cycles the moment you're called
  • Physical movement calms the nervous system — warm up actively

Visualization Protocol (Daily)

  • 5 minutes, eyes closed, after waking up
  • See the collar tie, feel the snap, feel the go-behind
  • Feel the seatbelt lock in. Feel the RNC tighten.
  • Hear the tap. Stand up. Reset.
  • Do 3 full match visualizations this way per week

During the Match

  • When in doubt: collar tie. Return to your first move.
  • One scramble at a time — forget what just happened
  • If you get submitted: reset mentally. You have time left. Use it.
  • Don't watch the clock — it slows down when you do
  • If you're tired: slow your pace, not your technique

Pre-Match Checklist

  • Arrive 45 min early — find your mat, watch the matches
  • Warm up 20 min before your call time
  • Mentally rehearse your collar tie sequence one time
  • Set one intention only: "Get to the back."
  • Controlled breath as they call your name — you've done this 1000 times in practice
